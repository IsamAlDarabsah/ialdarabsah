About

Biological systems rarely respond to the present alone. A population matures, an infection incubates, a signal travels down an axon — and by the time the effect arrives, the cause has moved on. Building that lag into a model changes what the model can do.

I use nonlinear ordinary and delay differential equations to study problems from immunology, epidemiology, ecology, and neural networks. The questions I care about are the qualitative ones: when an equilibrium loses stability, what the system does afterwards, and which biological quantity is really driving the transition. The tools are dynamical systems theory, perturbation methods, and numerical continuation.

Before joining JUST I was a PIMS Postdoctoral Fellow at the University of Manitoba and held a postdoctoral position in Applied Mathematics at the University of Waterloo. I completed my PhD at Memorial University of Newfoundland.
